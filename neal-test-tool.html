The Tool

What is the Neal Test?

The Neal Test has two non-negotiable requirements:

The Formula
Manual Service Delivery
+
Customer Payment
=
Real Validation

You personally deliver the service your product would automate. No technology. No automation. Just you doing the work.

And they pay you for it.

1/7

The Problem

Why founders avoid this

I built this tool because founders kept doing the same thing: building features for months without talking to users.

When I'd say "just manually deliver the service first," they'd respond with:

  • "But how exactly do I do that?"
  • "That sounds vague and hard"
  • "I don't know where to start"

The Neal Test was too abstract. Founders needed concrete scenarios with specific steps.

2/7

The Solution

How the tool works

You give the tool two inputs:

  1. Startup description - What you're building, who it's for, what problem you're solving
  2. Current product idea - The specific thing you're considering building

The tool analyzes your inputs and generates 5 fully customized Neal Test scenarios with:

  • Exact pitch scripts you can copy-paste
  • Daily routines with specific time blocks
  • Pricing suggestions based on your service type
  • Where to find your target customers
  • Success metrics for each scenario

The Trade-off

Concrete vs flexible

Design Decision
‚ùå Generic
"Go talk to users and manually deliver your service"
‚úÖ Specific
"Text 5 founders this pitch by Wednesday: '[exact script]'"

I chose specificity over flexibility.

The tool makes assumptions about your business model, pricing, and customer type. Sometimes it's wrong.

But a concrete wrong scenario is better than vague right advice. You can adjust specifics. You can't execute vague.

The Pattern

Tools that remove friction

"The Neal Test tool doesn't validate your idea for you. It removes the friction that stops you from validating it yourself."

Before this tool: "I should probably talk to users... but how?"

After this tool: "Here are 5 ways to start today. Pick one."

That's the pattern. Turn abstract methodology into concrete next steps.
